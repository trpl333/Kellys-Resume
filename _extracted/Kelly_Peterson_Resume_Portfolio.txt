Chino Unified School District  |  Anna Borba Elementary
SITUATION
Two students at Anna Borba Elementary were consistently labeled "behavior problems" — low trust in school, inconsistent support at home, and risk factors actively working against their engagement and academic trajectory.
ACTIONS
Invested consistent time and structured daily check-ins to earn trust before increasing academic expectations — relationship-first, not compliance-first.
Identified and addressed basic-needs barriers (materials access, food insecurity signals) to remove obstacles to participation and learning.
Used interest-based engagement strategies to build self-efficacy, strengthen school connection, and shift the students' own narrative about what they were capable of.
RESULTS
Both students showed measurable improvement in classroom engagement and participation; family-school partnership deepened significantly, with caregivers becoming active participants in school events for the first time in years.
Years later, both students credited the consistent support as a key factor in avoiding negative outcomes and establishing stable paths forward.
WHY IT MATTERS
Demonstrates relationship-first classroom management, crisis-resistant instructional decision-making, and the capacity to produce long-horizon impact — well beyond short-term compliance metrics.
STORY CARD B: CONFIDENCE-TO-COMPETENCE LITERACY GROWTH
Mission Hills Christian School  |  Junior High English
SITUATION
A student arrived with deep, entrenched reading avoidance. The parent stated directly that the student "hated reading" and was not expected to become a reader — framing the outcome as already decided before instruction began.
ACTIONS
Shifted the instructional focus from performance pressure to achievable, scaffolded wins — prioritizing confidence repair over immediate grade-level benchmarks.
Designed reading experiences where early effort produced visible success, systematically building a track record the student could point to and trust.
Reinforced a reader identity consistently across two full academic years: "you can do this" framing, not remediation framing.
RESULTS
Student transitioned from active reading avoidance to genuine engagement and self-directed reading interest.
In high school, the student independently chose to enroll in novel-study elective coursework and credited the confidence foundation built earlier as the reason they were willing to try.
WHY IT MATTERS
Demonstrates instructional patience, mindset-change capability, and the ability to produce durable academic identity shifts. This is a literacy intervention result achieved without a formal SPED designation — proof of high-quality Tier 1/2 differentiation in action.
